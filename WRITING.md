# Blog Writing Style — AmaQithi

These rules apply whenever writing or editing blog posts for this site. Read them before drafting anything.

---

## Voice

First person. The author is present. Use "I" when sharing a perspective, noticing something, or being honest about uncertainty. Not every sentence — but enough that a reader knows a person wrote this.

The author is Ayabonga Qwabi. The site is about his clan's history. The writing should feel like someone deeply inside the subject, not an outside commentator.

---

## Tone

Conversational but not casual. Like explaining something fascinating to a friend who is smart but not a specialist. No jargon without explanation. No performative enthusiasm.

Allowed: dry humour, genuine curiosity, open speculation clearly labelled as speculation.

Not allowed: cheerleading, hype, "amazing", "fascinating", "incredible", exclamation marks in body text, rhetorical questions used as transitions.

---

## Uncertainty

When the research is unresolved, say so plainly. "We do not know yet." "This is a theory, not a fact." "The written record has not confirmed it." Do not soften uncertainty with hedging language that sounds confident. Do not overstate it either.

---

## Structure

- Open on the thing itself, not on context about the thing. No "In today's world..." or "Throughout history..." openers.
- Short paragraphs. Two to four sentences is enough. Let them breathe.
- Section headings (`h3`) when the topic shifts. Not more than three per post. No heading for the opening section.
- No bullet lists in the body. If something feels like a list, write it as prose.
- End on a thought, not a call to action. The last paragraph should earn its place. No "Share this", no "Let me know in the comments."

---

## Language specifics

- Do not put "the" before isiXhosa or isiZulu nouns. Write "izibongo", not "the izibongo". Write "ingqithi", not "the ingqithi". The words carry their own number and reference.
- Xhosa/Zulu/Nguni words are italicised on first use with a plain-English gloss in parentheses. After that, use them without italics.
- Clan and people names are not italicised: AmaQithi, AbaThwa, abaThembu, Mqithi, Qwabi.
- Dates are written in full: 20 November 1879, not 11/20/1879 or Nov 20 1879.
- Place names follow the local spelling where one exists: Rhodana, not Rodana; Quthing, not Koetjing.

---

## What to avoid

These are the patterns that make the writing feel AI-generated. Avoid all of them:

- Significance inflation: "testament to", "pivotal moment", "underscores the importance of", "marks a shift"
- Copula avoidance: "serves as", "stands as", "functions as" — just use "is"
- Hollow intensifiers: "truly", "deeply", "profoundly", "remarkably"
- Transition padding: "Moreover,", "Furthermore,", "It is worth noting that", "In conclusion,"
- Rule of three for its own sake: if you have listed three things and the third is just padding, cut it
- Generic closers: "The future looks bright", "Only time will tell", "One thing is certain"
- Bold overuse: bold for a term being introduced, or a name that needs to stand out. Not for emphasis on ordinary phrases.
- Em dash overuse: one per post maximum. Use a comma or a full stop instead.

---

## SEO (without compromising the writing)

- The title should be specific enough to answer a question or name a thing. Not "The History of X" but "Why the Q in AmaQithi is a San sound."
- The excerpt (used as meta description) should be one or two sentences that give the argument, not just the topic. A reader should know what they are getting.
- First paragraph should contain the primary subject naturally. Do not stuff keywords.
- Use the real names: AmaQithi, Mqithi, Rhodana, Quthing, Ingqithi, izibongo, abaThembu, AbaThwa. These are what people searching for this history will use.
