# Blog Writing Style — AmaQithi

These rules apply whenever writing or editing blog posts for this site. Read them before drafting anything.

---

## Voice

First person. The author is present. Use "I" when sharing a perspective, noticing something, or being honest about uncertainty. Not every sentence — but enough that a reader knows a person wrote this.

The author is Ayabonga Qwabi. The site is about his clan's history. The writing should feel like someone deeply inside the subject, not an outside commentator, and not a guide explaining the culture to outsiders (see Audience, below, for who exactly is reading).

Avoiding cliché is not the same as avoiding style. A turn of phrase that's slightly unexpected — even a little indulgent — is worth more than the flattest, fastest route from one idea to the next. The prose can have a personality: a favourite kind of sentence, a recurring image, a slightly stubborn way of putting things. Sounding careful is good. Sounding like nobody in particular wrote it is the actual failure. (None of this overrides Plain language, below — a personality can show up in plain words just fine.)

---

## Audience

Most readers are Xhosa, many are Thembu, many are young, many are from rural areas, and many are reading in English as a second language with entry-level to medium fluency. They did not come here for a vocabulary lesson — they came because it's their clan, their history, their name.

This site is also something people show each other more than something people sit and read start to finish. It needs to look like real, careful work — something a person can point to and say "look what we found" — without that work being dressed up in language that only impresses someone who already had the vocabulary going in.

Plain language is not the same as less serious, and it is not "dumbing down" the history. It's the difference between a post a sharp 16-year-old from a rural village can read all the way through without reaching for a dictionary, and one that only lands with people who studied this kind of thing at varsity.

---

## Plain language

If sophistication were rated 1 to 10, this should sit at about a 3. Knowledgeable, never bombastic. The facts and the research stay exactly as serious as they are — only the words carrying them get simpler.

- Reading level target: a community newspaper or a Grade 8-10 textbook, not a university paper or a literary blog. If in doubt, go simpler.
- Prefer the short, common word over the long, formal one: use, not utilize; show, not demonstrate; because, not due to the fact that; before, not prior to; started, not commenced; helped, not facilitated.
- Avoid words that need their own explanation: hermeneutics, discourse, narrative (as in "the narrative of..."), historiography, syncretic, ethnonym, vernacular, toponymy. If the "correct" academic word would need a definition, it's the wrong word — find the plain one, even if it takes a few more words to say the same thing.
- One idea per sentence, most of the time. If a sentence needs "which," "in that," or more than one comma to hold itself together, it's probably two sentences pretending to be one.
- Repeating a simple word ("said," "because," "and") is fine. Plain and repeated beats fancy and varied — see Synonym cycling, under What to avoid, which exists for the opposite reason but lands in the same place.
- isiXhosa words still get italicised and glossed once (see Language specifics, below) — but the gloss itself should be plain English, not a fancy word standing in for a fancy word.
- Read the paragraph out loud. If it sounds like it belongs in a lecture, rewrite it as if explaining it to a cousin at home who is sharp but didn't get the chance to study this.

---

## Tone

Conversational but not casual. Like explaining something interesting to a cousin at home — someone sharp and curious, with an average school-level command of English, not someone who studied this at varsity. Jargon gets a plain explanation right there in the same sentence, not assumed. No performative enthusiasm.

Allowed: dry humour (see Humour, below), genuine curiosity, open speculation clearly labelled as speculation.

Not allowed: cheerleading, hype, "amazing", "fascinating", "incredible", exclamation marks in body text, rhetorical questions used as transitions.

---

## Humour

Calibrate humour to communal Xhosa wit, not to British irony or American one-liners — and never let humour come at the expense of the people, rituals, or beliefs the post is describing.

- Self-deprecation only: if something earns a laugh, let it be the author's confusion, a wrong turn in the research, or the gap between what he expected to find and what the record says. Not the ancestors, not the ritual, not the belief.
- Humour as recognition, not mockery: a light, witty line about a person or clan figure should read as respect, the way a good toast does, not as a joke at their expense.
- The incongruent pivot: build a sentence on a reasonable expectation, then let the real answer swerve somewhere smaller, stranger, or more mundane than expected. The swerve carries the humour, not a setup-punchline structure.
- Concrete over abstract: where the instinct is to reach for an intensifier ("remarkably," "incredibly"), reach instead for one precise, earthy detail. The detail does the work the adverb was trying to do, and it lands better.
- Names carry their own characterization: a clan name or nickname is often already a description, not just a label. Gloss its literal meaning plainly, once, and let it do the work. Don't explain why it's fitting or funny.
- A proverb, once, can replace a sentence of explanation: a Xhosa saying used as an anchor for an idea earns its place if it's doing real work, not decorating the page.
- The inquit: when reporting how someone spoke or reacted, a precise verb does more than "said" ever could. One well-chosen verb in an attribution carries characterization and a flicker of wit, without needing an extra sentence of commentary.
- Gravity and levity sit beside each other, not instead of each other: a small human or absurd detail placed right next to a historical event's weight sharpens the weight rather than undercutting it.
- One wry beat per post is enough. This is a history with a visible author, not a comedy piece.
- Whatever device is used above, it still has to come out in plain, everyday words (see Plain language). A clever idea explained in only words a Grade 9 learner already knows beats a less clever idea dressed up in long ones.

## Reasoning

A claim without a why or a how attached to it is just an assertion, and the reader is left to either trust it or skip it. Every "what" needs a thread of "why" or "how" running through it — not an essay, just enough that the logic doesn't float free of itself.

- If a name carries a meaning, give the meaning and where it points. Don't just claim it's "significant."
- If one event leads to another, show what caused what, rather than stating the outcome and trusting the reader to fill in the chain.
- If a claim rests on a source, name the source near the claim itself, not in a vague gesture at the end of a paragraph ("historians believe," "it is widely held that").
- Give the why or how in the plainest words available. An explanation written in dense language defeats its own purpose — it's meant to make things clearer, not prove how much was read to write it.
- Leaving a question open is fine (see Uncertainty, below). Leaving a stated fact unexplained, when the explanation is short and available, is not.

---

## Uncertainty

When the research is unresolved, say so plainly. "We do not know yet." "This is a theory, not a fact." "The written record has not confirmed it." Do not soften uncertainty with hedging language that sounds confident. Do not overstate it either.

Do not import a narrative shape that the record doesn't support. If the history is messy, anticlimactic, or simply stops without a resolution, let it stop. Resist the pull toward a tidy arc, a moral, or a redemptive turn that the events themselves don't earn.

---

## Structure

- Open on the thing itself, not on context about the thing. No "In today's world..." or "Throughout history..." openers.
- Short paragraphs. Two to four sentences is enough. Let them breathe.
- Hold the rhythm for the whole piece, not just the opening. A long run of short, identical sentences ("He paused. He frowned. He left.") is a collapse, not a style — vary sentence length a little, but lean toward shorter and clearer as the default. Rhythm should come from voice and word choice, not from longer or more complex sentences.
- Write to the length the material earns. If the research and the story support a long post, let it run long. Do not compress, summarize, or pad to fit an assumed "normal" blog-post length.
- Section headings (`h3`) when the topic shifts. Not more than three per post. No heading for the opening section.
- No excessive bullet lists. For narrative posts, write lists as prose. For info pieces where a reader needs to scan or look something up, bullet points are allowed — keep them short and factual, not padded.
- End on a thought, not a call to action. The last paragraph should earn its place. No "Share this," no "Let me know in the comments."

## Language specifics

- Do not put "the" before isiXhosa or isiZulu nouns. Write "izibongo", not "the izibongo". Write "ingqithi", not "the ingqithi". The words carry their own number and reference.
- Xhosa/Zulu/Nguni words are italicised on first use with a plain-English gloss in parentheses. After that, use them without italics.
- Clan and people names are not italicised: AmaQithi, AbaThwa, abaThembu, Mqithi, Qwabi.
- Dates are written in full: 20 November 1879, not 11/20/1879 or Nov 20 1879.
- Place names follow the local spelling where one exists: Rhodana, not Rodana; Quthing, not Koetjing.
- Do not say "the dead" when referring to ancestors — it is disrespectful. Use *abaphantsi*  or *abalele ukthula* . Gloss on first use, then use with italics.

## What to avoid

Most of these patterns do the same thing: they trade a specific, checkable fact for a generic, confident-sounding one — a sharp detail fading into a flattering blur while the sentence around it gets louder about how important it is. They also overlap heavily with Plain language, above — most AI-isms are accidentally also the fanciest way to say something simple. If a sentence sounds impressive but there's no specific fact underneath it, find the fact and use it, or cut the sentence.

- Significance inflation: "testament to," "pivotal moment," "underscores the importance of," "marks a shift"
- Copula avoidance: "serves as," "stands as," "functions as" — just use "is"
- Hollow intensifiers: "truly," "deeply," "profoundly," "remarkably"
- Transition padding: "Moreover," "Furthermore," "It is worth noting that," "In conclusion,"
- Manufactured drama: "But here's the thing," "Here's why that matters," "The result?," "Hot take:," "And honestly?" — these stage tension instead of just saying the next sentence
- Filler phrases: "in order to" (to), "due to the fact that" (because), "at this point in time" (now), "it is important to note that" (delete)
- Vague attributions: "experts say," "historians believe," "industry reports," "critics argue" — name the actual source close to the claim, or cut the claim
- Synonym cycling: renaming the same subject repeatedly for variety ("the warrior... the chief... the man...") instead of just using the name
- False ranges: "from X to Y" pairings where X and Y aren't actually points on a meaningful scale
- Negative parallelism: "not only X but Y," "it's not just about X, it's Y" — say the one thing directly
- Superficial -ing tails: a gerund clause stapled onto a sentence to fake analysis ("...highlighting the resilience of...," "...underscoring how..."). If the analysis is worth making, give it its own sentence and its own reason
- Rule of three for its own sake: if you have listed three things and the third is just padding, cut it
- Generic closers: "The future looks bright," "Only time will tell," "One thing is certain"
- Bold overuse: bold for a term being introduced, or a name that needs to stand out. Not for emphasis on ordinary phrases
- Em dash overuse: one per post maximum. Use a comma or a full stop instead
- Imported irony: dry, biting sarcasm aimed at elders, rituals, or belief — reads as outsider mockery, not insider wit, however clever it sounds
- Stock attribution verbs: defaulting to the same reporting verb for every quote, or swapping it for a thesaurus synonym purely for variety. Use the verb that's actually true to the moment (see the inquit, under Humour), and let plain "said" do its job the rest of the time

## SEO (without compromising the writing)

- The title should be specific enough to answer a question or name a thing. Not "The History of X" but "Why the Q in AmaQithi is a San sound."
- The excerpt (used as meta description) should be one or two sentences that give the argument, not just the topic. A reader should know what they are getting.
- First paragraph should contain the primary subject naturally. Do not stuff keywords.
- Use the real names: AmaQithi, Mqithi, Rhodana, Quthing, Ingqithi, izibongo, abaThembu, AbaThwa. These are what people searching for this history will use.
