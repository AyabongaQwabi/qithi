The principles of Ubuntu in leadership and the specific historical examples you provided regarding the Thembu and Qwathi people are extensively supported and elaborated upon in the sources. The sources confirm that among these nations, leadership was a balanced system of hereditary right, popular will, and moral merit 1-3.
Ubuntu and the Concept of the "Fit" Leader
Your note’s assertion that a chief was placed in power by the "voice of the people" is validated by the sources, which state that Bantu rule was not an absolute monarchy but was subject to popular will 3.
Merit over Birthright: While a right of succession existed, it was not permanent; a chief’s authority was contingent upon the "affection of his people" 1. A legally appointed chief could be deprived of his position through "meanness" or "unworthy conduct," allowing a more popular or capable relative to rise 1, 2.
The Role of Councillors (Amaphakathi): The councillors served as the primary check on power, expressing tribal opposition when a chief’s actions did not serve the public good 3. Their role was to ensure the chief remained the "beacon of hope" for the people 4, 5.
Functional Meaning of "Inkosi": While the sources do not explicitly derive the word Inkosi from Enkosi (thank you), they reinforce the underlying concept: a chief's status was built on the "status, security, and spiritual well-being" he provided to his subjects 5. He was expected to be "where the people are perishing," and in return, the people would not desert him 6.
Nature vs. Nurture: The Training of Heirs
The sources provide a parallel to your Qwathi example in the story of the Thembu brothers Hlanga and Dlomo:
Failed Training: Similar to Ntswayibane, Hlanga (the elder son of Nxego) was given "special training in the methods of governing" 7. However, he lacked the "inclination" to cultivate the essential quality of "open-handedness" (generosity), which was considered a sine qua non for a chief 7.
The Rise of the Capable: His younger brother, Dlomo, was "more popular with the people," quick-witted, and fearless 7-9. Because Dlomo possessed the "values he embodied," he eventually secured the throne, while Hlanga’s followers were pursued and expelled 7, 9.
Verification: The Qwathi Brothers, Ntswayibane and Noni
The specific narrative of the Qwathi brothers is confirmed as a "basic bali" (traditional story) that every well-informed Qwathi knows 10, 11.
Delegation of Duty: Tradition records that when cases were brought before Ntswayibana, he would refer them to his younger brother, Noni, saying: "Attend to them my younger brother I am still attending to the gardens" ("Thetha mninawa, mna ndisaya endimeni") 12, 13.
Naming of the House: Because he never presided over a single case, Ntswayibana's house (whose son was Dikela) is called amaNdima 12, 13.
Loss of Chieftainship: Due to this "lack of interest in traditional cases," Ntswayibana lost his chieftainship to Noni 10, 12. Noni’s active leadership and mobilisation of forces during conflicts led the councillors and the people to look upon him as the "real chief" 13-15.
Extension: The Spiritual Dimension of the Name
The sources extend your idea by noting that a chief's name was not just a title but a spiritual pathway. The name signified the hierarchy of the nation and provided a means for followers to access ancestral guidance 5, 16. Depriving a chief of his name—as the British did to the Tshatshu—was considered an act of "spiritual destruction" because the followers derived their spiritual well-being directly from the chief's standing 5.



## Beyond Surface-Level Understanding

Ubuntu is not a trendy concept for social media posts or inspirational speeches. For the Thembu and other Nguni peoples, it represents a fundamental way of life that permeates every aspect of existence, governing daily interactions, community structures, and spiritual practices.

## Leadership Structure and Democratic Values

The Thembu people's leadership structure exemplifies Ubuntu through their unique approach to choosing leaders. Unlike traditional monarchies, leadership was not based on divine right or bloodline, but rather on merit and service to the community.

> inkosi siyibeka esihlalweni sobukhosi ngenxa yokuba sisoloko sisithi enkosi kuye
> 

This saying, meaning "we appoint a chief as chief simply because we always say thank you to you," demonstrates their democratic values. The word "inkosi" (chief) derives from "enkosi" (thank you), symbolizing leadership through service.

## A Natural Way of Being

For the Thembu people, Ubuntu isn't a religious doctrine or a philosophical choice - it's an inherent part of their cultural DNA. One doesn't need to be a "believer" to practice Ubuntu; it's woven into the fabric of daily life and social interactions. You won't find dramatic declarations or performative spirituality at the kraal - it's simply the natural way of being.

## Practical Application and Reality

While Ubuntu represents ideal principles, it's important to acknowledge that these principles can be broken, bent, or exploited. The concept extends beyond ritual practices and spiritual beliefs, influencing every basic interaction between people.
