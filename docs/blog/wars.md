### **The Blood-Stained Soil: A History of War in the Eastern Cape**

The Eastern Cape has historically been a landscape of intense contestation, where the expansion of **Bantu nations**, the residency of the **abaThembu**, and the ancestral hunting grounds of the **San** collided with colonial ambitions. The sources reveal a history defined by "recurrent affrays" and major conflicts that shaped the identities of these nations.

#### **The Nine Frontier Wars: Bantu Resistance Against Colonial Advance**
The most documented conflicts are the series of wars between the Xhosa (Bantu) nations and the European colonists, often referred to as the Frontier Wars:
*   **First Frontier War (1779–1781):** Triggered by land and stock tensions between the Xhosa and encroaching Boers; the **imiDange** and **amaMbalu** were heavily involved.
*   **Second Frontier War (1793):** Involving the **amaGqunukhwebe**, **amaMbalu**, and the **amaNdlambe** as they resisted subordination.
*   **Third Frontier War (1799):** An attempt by the British to expel the Xhosa from disputed territory.
*   **Fourth Frontier War (1811–1812):** The first time the Colonial Government displayed its full military strength to expel **Ndlambe** and **Chungwa**.
*   **Fifth Frontier War (1818–1819):** Famous for the **Battle of Grahamstown**, where the prophet **Nxele** (Makanda) led a daylight attack.
*   **Sixth Frontier War (1834–1835):** Also known as the **War of Hintsa**, sparked by the wounding of Xoxo; it led to the death and mutilation of the Gcaleka King, **Hintsa**.
*   **Seventh Frontier War (1846–1847):** The **War of the Axe**, caused when chiefs refused to hand over a thief; it saw the involvement of **Maphasa** of the **amaTshatshu**.
*   **Eighth Frontier War (1850–1853):** The **War of Mlanjeni**, described as the bloodiest; the **amaTshatshu** under Maphasa and the **amaNgqika** under Sandile fought a determined guerrilla campaign.
*   **Ninth Frontier War (1877–1878):** The **War of Ngcayecibi**, which began as a scuffle at a wedding and eventually led to the imprisonment of the Tshatshu chief, **Gungubele**.

#### **Thembu Conflicts: Defending Sovereignty**
The **abaThembu** were often viewed as "least warlike," yet they fought significant battles to maintain their autonomy:
*   **Battle of Msana (c. 1650):** An internal battle for supremacy between the brothers **Hlanga** and **Dlomo**.
*   **War with Rarabe (1770):** The Xhosa chief **Rharhabe** met his death in a conflict with the Thembu under **Ndaba**.
*   **Wars with the amaBhaca:** Fought by **Ngubencuka** to protect his borders from encroaching Bhaca groups.
*   **Battle of Mbholompo (1828):** A joint force of Thembu, Xhosa, and British troops crushed the **amaNgwane** (Matiwane) who had invaded Thembuland.
*   **War of Nongxakazelo (1875):** A war between the Xhosa (**Sarhili**) and the Thembu (**Ngangelizwe**) following the brutal treatment of Sarhili’s daughter by her husband, the Thembu King.
*   **The Qwathi Rebellion (1880–1881):** A final anti-colonial uprising where the **amaQwathi** resisted being handed over to British rule by Ngangelizwe.

#### **San (Bushman) Wars: A Fight for Survival**
The **San** nations maintained an "unconquerable spirit," fighting to protect their ancestral hunting grounds:
*   **Dutch-San Conflicts (1650s onwards):** Early fighting broke out as the Dutch disregarded San land rights, leading to decades of raiding and counter-raiding.
*   **The General Commando (1774):** A coordinated, three-pronged colonial attack designed to destroy San resistance across the frontier.
*   **The Rharhabe-San War:** A prolonged struggle sparked when the San stole King **Rharhabe’s** favorite ox; Rharhabe eventually destroyed the responsible band in their rocky stronghold.
*   **The "Battle of Blood":** A desperate engagement between the San and the **Batlapin**, remembered for the high casualties inflicted by San poisoned arrows.
*   **Wars with the Koranas and Griquas:** As groups like the **Koranas** and **Griquas** moved north of the Orange River, they engaged in constant warfare with the San over territory and cattle.

### **The amaBhaca: A Nation of Warriors**
The **amaBhaca**, particularly under the leadership of **Madikane**, were a formidable force driven south by the migrations of the *Mfecane*.
*   **Wars with the amaMpondomise:** Upon reaching Bencuti (Shawbury) around 1825, the Bhaca attacked the Mpondomise, fighting for three days until the Mpondomise were forced to give ground.
*   **Conflict with the Thembu and Xhosa:** The Bhaca attacked the **amaTshatshu** and **amaGcina Thembu**, causing the Tshatshu to flee to Maqoma for safety. This led to a combined force of Xhosa, Thembu, and Mpondomise crushing the Bhaca and killing Madikane.
*   **Pondo-Bhaca Wars:** Later, under **Ncapayi**, the Bhaca engaged in frequent raids against the **amaMpondo**. In 1845, a powerful Mpondo force under Faku finally defeated the Bhaca, resulting in Ncapayi's death.
*   **The 1840 Boer-Ntlangwini Raid:** A commando of Boers and **Ntlangwini** attacked Ncapayi’s Bhaca in Pondoland to "punish" them for alleged cattle theft, resulting in the capture of many cattle and people.

### **The amaMpondomise: Internal and External Strife**
The **amaMpondomise** history is marked by both defensive wars and internal succession struggles.
*   **Succession Wars:** A significant conflict erupted between the rightful heir **Mhlontlo** and his uncle **Mditshwa (Gqirana)** over the chieftainship.
*   **Joyi’s War (c. 1680/Traditional History):** A war broke out between the Thembu under **Joyi** and the Mpondomise under Gqirana following insults by a praise-poet (*imbongi*). The Mpondomise utilized a "charmed" pool to defeat the Thembu, forcing them to retreat.
*   **Hope’s War (The Rebellion of 1881):** Triggered by the colonial **Disarmament Act**, Mhlontlo led an uprising against the British. He famously assassinated the magistrate, **Hamilton Hope**, and joined forces with the **Basutos** and **amaQwathi** in a desperate attempt to resist colonial rule.

### **The amaMpondo: Defending the Coast**
The **amaMpondo** under King **Faku** were central to the politics of the eastern frontier.
*   **The Mpondo-Bomvana War:** Shortly after 1800, the Mpondo Paramount **Ngqungqushe** was killed in a war against the **amaBomvana** led by Gambushe.
*   **War with the Xesibe:** The Mpondo army, led by **Mbangazita**, was "hopelessly beaten" in a battle against the **amaXesibe**, who were assisted by Bhaca fugitives.
*   **The Zulu Invasions:** The Mpondo territory was repeatedly overrun by **Tshaka’s** Zulu regiments. They successfully used their steep, coastal terrain to hide cattle and resist total subjugation.

### **The amaHlubi: The Struggle for Survival**
The **amaHlubi** were one of the most powerful tribes in Natal before being broken by the *Mfecane*.
*   **Wars with the amaNgwane:** The Hlubi under **Mtimkulu II** and **Mpangazita** fought a series of desperate, multi-day battles against **Matiwane’s** amaNgwane. In the final five-day battle near present-day Maseru (1825), the Hlubi power was broken, and Mpangazita was killed.
*   **Conflict with the Thembu:** The Hlubi under **Ludidi** were at one point attacked and forced to move by the Thembu chief **Joyi**.

### **The Koranas: Raiders of the Interior**
The **Koranas** (a Khoi group) were influential raiders who utilized firearms to dominate the middle veld.
*   **Wars with the San:** The Koranas engaged in a "first Bushman war" at a drift called **'Go-'koo-lume**, named for the merciless way the San were shot down. This began a cycle of raiding and revenge that lasted decades.
*   **Conflicts with Bantu Nations:** The Koranas launched treacherous attacks on the **Barolong** and **Basuto**, often driving these nations from their land to seize cattle.
*   **The War of 1868:** A period of "lawless freebooting" along the Orange River erupted into war as Korana groups, Xhosa immigrants, and Bastaards fought over dwindling water and grazing land during a severe drought.

### **The amaMfengu (Fingoes): Colonial Allies and Combatants**
While often described as refugees, the **amaMfengu** played a pivotal military role.
*   **Resistance to Xhosa Overlordship:** Settled on Xhosa lands, the Mfengu eventually resisted the political control of the Xhosa chiefs.
*   **The War of Ngcayecibi (1877–1878):** This "Gcaleka War" began specifically as a clash between the amaMfengu and the **amaGcaleka** at a wedding feast, which eventually drew in the British and the entire frontier.

### **Other Notable Conflicts**
*   **The amaNgqosini War:** This tribe of Basuto origin tried to overthrow the **amaTshawe** (Xhosa royals) but failed. They were later involved in a war with the Gcaleka Xhosa, who utilized **San archers** with poisoned arrows to kill the Ngqosini chief, **Mjobi**.
*   **The Battle of Pramberg (1851):** A conflict between Boers and the **cis-Orange Xhosa** (including groups under Kort Klaas) over grazing rights during a drought.
