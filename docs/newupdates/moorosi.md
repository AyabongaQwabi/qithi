The relationship between the Phuthi chief **Moorosi** and the **San** people (AbaThwa) was one of the deepest and most enduring indigenous alliances in 19th-century Southern Africa, characterized by **intermarriage, military collaboration, and a shared artistic heritage**.

### **Lineage and Marital Ties**
Moorosi’s personal identity and political authority were deeply rooted in San lineage.
*   **San Ancestry:** Moorosi was reported to be of **San descent on his mother's side**. His father, Mokuoane, formalised a Phuthi-San alliance by marrying the sister of the San chief **Quu**. 
*   **Physical Appearance:** Contemporary observers noted that Moorosi’s appearance reflected this heritage; he was described as **small, yellow-skinned, and very short**.
*   **His Own Households:** Moorosi continued this tradition by having **two San wives**. By at least 1869, many San lived in huts amongst Moorosi’s Phuthi, tending cattle and sheep and cultivating crops, suggesting a high degree of social integration.

### **Patronage of San Art and Rituals**
Moorosi was a significant patron of the San’s ritual and artistic practices.
*   **Witness to Art:** Moorosi is one of the few historical figures recorded to have **witnessed San artists at work**. He visited the San leader **Soai** at **Sehonghong Cave**, where he saw three painters with white goatee beards working on the cave walls.
*   **Shared Painting:** Moorosi’s son, **Mopote**, reportedly **painted with the San**, as did Moorosi’s "half-Bush" stepbrothers.
*   **Rainmaking:** Moorosi maintained close ties with **Raisa**, a principal chief and war doctor who was also a "great friend of the San" and likely drew on their rainmaking expertise.

### **Military and Raiding Alliances**
The San were essential military allies to Moorosi, particularly in his resistance to colonial and Sotho expansion.
*   **Joint Raids:** Between 1868 and 1870, the majority of cattle raids in Natal and the Cape Colony were conducted by **combined parties of San and Phuthi**. Stolen horses were typically divided between the two groups, while cattle were taken back to Phuthi villages.
*   **The Last San Chief:** Moorosi provided a safe haven for **Soai**, the last major Maloti San leader. Soai frequently visited Moorosi’s place, and many of his followers joined Moorosi after Soai was killed by Sotho forces in 1871.

### **Moorosi’s War (1879)**
The 1879 rebellion against the British government and the Basotho monarchy was the final stand for this alliance.
*   **San Combatants:** San fighters, **armed with bows and arrows**, fought alongside Moorosi’s forces. They were instrumental in inflicting heavy losses on Colonial troops near **Pokane** on the Senqu River.
*   **The Password:** The deep significance of the San to the Phuthi was demonstrated during the eight-month siege of **Mount Moorosi**. The password used by Phuthi warriors to access the mountain stronghold was **"Moroa"**, which means **"Bushman"**.
*   **Supply Lines:** During the siege, Moorosi utilized a San-style supply chain, **sending herds of cattle into the Maloti highlands** to be kept by San allies and re-stocking the rebels as needed.
*   **The End of an Era:** The fall of Mount Moorosi in November 1879 and the subsequent death of Moorosi effectively ended the autonomous existence of the south-eastern San, who lost their **most powerful protector and ally**.

### **Complexity of Relations**
While Moorosi was their greatest ally, the sources also note that he was a complex figure who sometimes **clashed with San bands** that were not allied with him and raided his own cattle. One account even states he destroyed entire clans to possess their women, though he is primarily remembered as a fierce protector of those who submitted to his rule.
