The historical record of the **White Kei River** (also known as the **Cacadu River**) and the San leader **Chief Madolo** (also known as **Madura** or **Madoor**) provides a detailed look at the final era of autonomous San leadership in the Eastern Cape during the mid-19th century.

### **Leadership and Territory of Chief Madolo**
*   **Origins and Power:** Madolo was the chief of one of the two most powerful San tribes in the region (the other being under Chief Mada'kane). Before 1835, his people occupied the territory around the **Klipplaats and Upper Black Kei rivers**. 
*   **Move to Glen Grey:** In approximately **1835**, Madolo retired with his people to the **Glen Grey** area, north of the Black Kei, in the vicinity of present-day **Lady Frere**. 
*   **The Bushman School:** In **1839**, the missionary **James Read** established an LMS outstation for the San on the **White Kei (Cacadu) River** called the **Bushman School**. By 1842, fifteen San families lived there, and Madolo frequently attended services in the small chapel, though he never personally converted to Christianity. 
*   **Appearance:** In 1848, the artist **Thomas Baines** described Madolo as a "diminutive old man, with meagre visage, prominent cheek bones and bare head".

### **Military Service and Colonial Betrayal**
*   **War of the Axe (1846):** During this conflict, Madolo led **200 followers**—a mixed force of San, Khoe, Mfengu, and Thembu—as **levies for the British**. They assisted the British in defeating the Xhosa.
*   **The "Unreasonable" Quitrent:** After the war, the Colonial government proclaimed the region part of the Cape Colony and demanded a **quitrent of one pound per year** from each family. Madolo protested, arguing his people were the ancient owners of the land and had already provided three years of military service, but his pleas were ignored.

### **Conflict and Displacement (1850–1856)**
*   **The Retreat to Caves:** Around **1850**, colonial pressure and conflict with an immigrant Thembu chief named **Ndhela** forced Madolo and another San chief, **Flux**, to flee the mission. They took refuge in **caves along the banks of the White Kei**.
*   **Cave of the Springbok:** One of these retreats, situated opposite the present St. Mark’s mission station, became known as the **"Cave of the Springbok"** due to a large symbolic painting of approximately 150 springboks.
*   **Final Retreat:** In **1856**, at approximately 80 years old, Madolo and the "shattered wreck" of his tribe retreated further into the fastnesses of the **Maloti-Drakensberg**, after which they disappeared from the historical record.

### **Cultural Context on the White Kei**
*   **San-Thembu Relations:** Before the conflicts of 1850, many Thembu groups on the White Kei, including the chief **Jumba**, were on friendly terms with local San families. The San acted as hunters, cattle-guards, and "rain doctors" for the Thembu.
*   **Artistic Legacy:** The White Kei basin was a hub of San artistic activity. Madolo’s cave featured extensive paintings of springboks, and an old San painter named **‘Gcu-wa** (brother of the chief Mada’kane) was recorded still living in the area as late as **1869**, carrying paint pots on his belt.
