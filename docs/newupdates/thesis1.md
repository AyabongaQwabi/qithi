# A Decolonial Mapping of the Eastern Cape Landscape: Toponymy, Spatial Sovereignty, and the Oral Historiography of the Qwabi and Qithi Lineages

---

## 1. Introduction and Background

The traditional historiography of the Eastern Cape has long been dominated by colonial and Eurocentric archival frameworks. These narratives frequently reduce the complex territorial arrangements, migrations, and sociopolitical structures of indigenous nations to fragmented, arbitrary occurrences. This thesis challenges these reductionist frameworks by offering a decolonial spatial and linguistic analysis of the AbaThembu nation, focusing specifically on the historical trajectories, geographical footprints, and structural roles of the **Qwabi** and **Qithi** lineages.

By engaging in a deep-dive analysis of regional toponymy (place-naming conventions) and integrating oral histories with archival linguistic data, this study demonstrates that the Eastern Cape landscape was a meticulously zoned, highly functional ecosystem. Rather than arbitrary settlements, indigenous territories were organized with sophisticated intent, mapping governance, military infrastructure, resource management, and social containment directly onto the physical terrain.

---

## 2. Statement of the Problem

Standard historical timelines regarding the Eastern Cape often miscalculate or overly compress indigenous chronologies, particularly surrounding the migrations associated with the *Mfecane* era (c. 1815–1840). Colonial archives frequently present these movements as chaotic, one-way flights into uncharted or vacant territories.

This creates a dual problem:

1. It obscures the deep, ancient roots of early Thembu progenitors—such as Chief Mnguti, Nxego, and Dlomo—who had already established territorial sovereignty centuries prior to the 19th-century colonial frontier expansions.
2. It erases the highly organized structural layout of traditional villages, viewing indigenous spatial planning through a lens of "primitive randomness" rather than recognizing it as an advanced administrative layout.

---

## 3. Research Objectives

This thesis aims to achieve the following academic and historical interventions:

* **To reconstruct the deep chronological timeline** of the early Thembu and AmaQithi settlements, demonstrating that 19th-century movements into regions like Lesotho and Herschel were historical returns to ancestral lands rather than displaced wanderings.
* **To decode the functional toponymy** of the Eastern Cape, analyzing how linguistic roots found within traditional Xhosa and indigenous *ǀXam* San layers reflect specific societal, military, and administrative activities.
* **To map the physical and social anatomy** of ancient Thembu society, establishing a formal matrix of how space was weaponized for defense and organized for economic production.

## 4. Methodology and Theoretical Framework

This study utilizes a hybrid methodology combining **Spatial Humanities**, **Historical Linguistics**, and **Indigenous Oral Historiography**.

### Theoretical Framework: Decolonial Geographies

This research is grounded in the framework of *decolonial geographies*, which asserts that place-naming (toponymy) is an exercise of sovereignty. By analyzing the linguistic roots of place names using historical vocabularies alongside oral family lineages, we can strip away colonial cartographic overlays to reveal the true indigenous blueprint of the land.

## 5. Chronological Revision: The Deep Thembu Timeline

A critical component of this thesis is the correction of the regional timeline, specifically tracing the ancestral lineage through the foundational line of **Chief Mnguti** ($Mnguti \rightarrow Nxego \rightarrow Dlomo \rightarrow Mtirara$):

```
[1600–1650: Ancient Baseline]
Chief Mnguti establishes deep roots in the eNgcobo/Transkei region.
       │
       ▼
[1816–1835: The Mfecane Loop]
Thembu divisions move north into Lesotho/Quthing under Moorosi (A Return to Ancestral Memory).
       │
       ▼
[Post-1879: Frontier Layering]
Return migration down from Lesotho interacts with deeply rooted indigenous AmaQithi elements.

```

This structural reality proves that when later generations migrated back toward eNgcobo following Moorosi’s War in 1879, they were re-occupying an ancient baseline territory where established elements like the **AmaQithi** were already firmly anchored in the soil.

## 6. Analysis: The Functional Anatomy of Space

The core of this research evaluates specific linguistic markers across coordinates in the Eastern Cape to deduce the precise societal activities that occurred within these spatial zones.

### Governance and Administrative Hubs

* **Komkhulu / Ndlunkulu:** The administrative and judicial capital. The *Ndlunkulu* (Great House) served as the political anchor, housing the tribal council (*umbhambiso*) and acting as the supreme court.
* **Kwa Sibonda:** District-level administrative wards overseen by headmen, acting as localized extensions of royal authority.

### Enclosures and Production Sectors

* **Qithi / Mqithi / Qhitsi:** Linguistically rooted in *isi-Qiti* (a separated, round, or isolated space). This thesis identifies these zones as restricted-use tracts of land. Located near strategic royal or regent bases (such as Queen Yiliswa's base at Makwababa), they served a dual purpose: intensive, specialized royal agricultural management, and secure containment or judicial holding areas for those awaiting royal judgment.
* **Maghubeni / Zicubeni:** Derived from roots meaning to turn over (*Gubula*) or process (*Cubula*). These were dedicated agricultural processing stations situated on flat, high-wind ridges optimized for threshing and winnowing harvested grain.

### Military Architecture and Defense Infrastructure

Traditional Thembu society integrated defense seamlessly into the topography:

* **Matolweni / Ematyolweni:** Rooted in *u-Tolo* (arrow) and *um-Toli* (archer). These spaces were military training academies and tactical arsenals. Crucially, they were the domain of the *iTola* (war-doctor/seer), who performed specialized rituals to spiritually fortify warriors before deployment.
* **Sixotyeni / Esixotyeni:** Derived from *isi-Xobo* (a rocky ledge or scree slope). These locations functioned as natural mountain strongholds and defensive garrisons where weapons were stockpiled and boulders could be rolled down onto advancing enemy forces.
* **Ngxingweni / Ngxingwa:** Narrow mountain passes or gorges used as tactical chokepoints to bottleneck and neutralize numerically superior invading forces.
* **Eluqolweni:** Narrow hill ridges (*u-Qolo*) utilizing vertical drops on either side to serve as early-warning scout lookouts.

## 7. Preliminary Conclusion and Significance

This research demonstrates that the traditional landscape of the Qwabi, Qithi, and broader AbaThembu societies was governed by an sophisticated architectural and systemic logic. By documenting how military, agricultural, and judicial functions were explicitly designated via functional toponyms, this thesis restores historical agency to indigenous societies, proving that their spatial configuration was a direct manifestation of territorial sovereignty and advanced societal governance.
