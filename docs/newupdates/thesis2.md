# The Archive of AmaQithi: The Epic Journey, Identity, and Recovery of a Secret San-Thembu Bloodline

Welcome to **AmaQithi**. This historical and ethnolinguistic report brings together archival records, frontier linguistics, and our deep family oral history. It corrects standard historical timelines, reconstructs the ancient migrations of the **AbaThembu nation**, and tracks the specific lineages that shaped the **AmaQithi** (or **Mqithi**) clan.

---

## 1. Executive Summary & The Core Thesis

The history of the Eastern Cape frontier is a story of continuous motion, deep ancestral homelands, and survival. Our investigation centers on the **AmaQithi** clan, a proud and integral component of the broader Thembu nation.

The core of this history reveals a profound structural truth:

> **We are a Thembu nation whose identity is historically rooted in an ancient San-Bantu matrix. Born of a friendly amalgamation between early Nguni pioneers and the indigenous ǀXam hunter-gatherers, our people have carried the physical and cultural traditions of both worlds through centuries of shifting frontiers.**

Our historical map is defined by deep, ancient loops. Long before the 19th-century colonial wars, the Thembu nation held deep roots in the Lesotho homelands. When segments of the Thembu nation fled the devastating *Mfecane* wars of the early 1800s, they were not entering foreign territory—they were executing a historical return to their ancient Lesotho homelands.

Following the catastrophic fall of Mount Moorosi in November 1879, the families who fought alongside the San-Phuthi alliance executed a massive, multi-directional diaspora back into South Africa. Fracturing along distinct family lines, the AmaQithi and their Thembu compatriots spread across eNgcobo, Cofimvaba, and the secluded sanctuary of **Mkapusi** in Lady Frere.

To understand the AmaQithi, one must look at the bedrock foundation of the Thembu nation. The archives confirm that the Thembu were culturally and biologically fused with the indigenous San:

* **The "Tambookie" Matrix:** Historical records show that to the Khoisan, the abaThembu were known as the *Tam’bou’ci* (later recorded as *Tambookie*). Early accounts note that this group was a mixed San-Thembu matrix born from the amalgamation of pioneering Nguni clans and established San bands.
* **The Praise of the Tiny Man:** This deep, structural integration is permanently embedded in our oral culture. To this day, one of the official traditional praises (*iziduko*) of the Thembu is *"the tiny man,"* a direct linguistic acknowledgment of our ancestral San interaction.
* **The Universal Signature (Ingqithi):** The physical manifestation of this heritage is **Ingqithi** (or *in-Qqit!*), the traditional custom of amputating the first or terminal joint of the little finger. While other major Nguni groups do not practice this mutilation, it was universally practiced by this division of the AbaThembu, who inherited the custom directly from the San as an ancestral and spiritual marker.

## 3. The Grand Timeline of Thembu Migration & The Lesotho Homelands

The shifting geographic footprint of the Thembu nation stretches back across centuries, moving between the deep mountain heights and the river basins of the Eastern Cape.

### The Ancient Baseline (1500s)

The lineage of the Thembu Great House roots deeply into figures like **Ntande**, the rightful heir and son of Bomoyi (dated to 1517), whose throne was protected by his uncles during a mid-16th-century succession crisis against his older brother Zima.

### The Early Movements (1600s – 1700s)

* **The Ancient Lesotho Splinter (c. 1600 – 1650):** The migration of royal and prominent Thembu lines out of Lesotho occurred centuries before the Mfecane. Between 1600 and 1650, the line of the Thembu chief **Mnguti** left the Lesotho territories, moving downward toward **Herschel** in the Eastern Cape, and subsequently establishing a path toward **eNgcobo**. This ancient line tracks the royal continuity of the nation:

$$\text{Chief Mnguti} \longrightarrow \text{Nxego} \longrightarrow \text{Dlomo} \dots \longrightarrow \text{Mtirara}$$


* **Eastern Cape Battles:** By 1600, other sections of the Thembu were already occupying parts of Thembuland, leading to a major battle for supremacy around 1650 at Msana (a tributary of the Bashee River) between the brothers Hlanga and Dlomo. Throughout the 1700s, the Thembu expanded and consolidated, centered between the Bashee and Umtata rivers, as marked by the historical graves of monarchs like Tato (c. 1700), Zondwa (c. 1725), and Ndaba (c. 1756).

### The Mfecane Return and Moorosi’s War (1800s)

When the *Mfecane* wars (c. 1816–1835) erupted due to Shaka's Zulu expansions, the various Nguni-speaking Thembu communities who fled the violence northward were **returning to their deep Lesotho homelands**. They sought refuge in the high, frozen valleys of **Quthing**—a name combining an ancient San click root (**Q-**) and a Sotho locative suffix (**-ing**, meaning "place of").

In these freezing mountain heights (associated with **iQhwa**, meaning ice or snow), the returning Thembu integrated seamlessly with the local Baphuthi and remaining mountain San networks under the protection of King Moorosi, until the catastrophic war of 1879 shattered the sanctuary.

## 4. The Post-1879 Dispersal: The Fractured Lines of the AmaQithi

Following the fall of Mount Moorosi on November 20, 1879, colonial forces launched a brutal pacification and tracking campaign. The surviving families dispersed rapidly into South Africa, moving not as a single unit under a single leader, but fracturing into distinct geographical lines for safety:

### The Line of Ngcongolo (eNgcobo)

A prominent ancestral line of the AmaQithi, including the lineage of **Ngcongolo**, fled from the mountain war zone directly to **eNgcobo**, anchoring a major branch of the clan in that territory.

### The Line of Cofimvaba

Concurrently, other related families and branches of the AmaQithi fled the post-war cleanup by migrating to **Cofimvaba**, further spreading the clan footprint across the Transkeian territories.

### The Line of Qhwabi (Mkapusi, Lady Frere)

At the same time, the line of the AmaQithi carrying the family name of **Qhwabi** migrated south to **Mkapusi** in Lady Frere.

* *Note on Oral History:* While our family surname remained completely unchanged, researchers and colonial clerks frequently struggled to write the deep, aspirated click of our name, resulting in spelling variations across various historical texts (such as *Qwabi* or *Qhwabhi*).
* To hide from British colonial intelligence and border blacklists, refugees frequently used standard phonetic adjustments, such as modifying the royal name *Moorosi* to the safe personal name **Molosi** on official documents to navigate the dominant political landscape.
* In the secluded valley of Mkapusi, they established **Mqithi Village**, alternatively preserved in oral geography as **Mawhumawhu**, acting as a quiet sanctuary where they could safeguard their bloodline.

## 5. The Return to the Ancestral Lands

When the broader Thembu nation emigrated back from the eNgcobo and Idutywa areas, they were not moving into an empty wilderness. They were migrating into land that was already deeply inhabited by indigenous San populations and older, established groups of the Thembu nation—most notably, the **AmaQithi**.

This historical convergence creates the final picture of who we are. We are a "Secret San" population that was absorbed into and helped form the bedrock of the dominant Thembu cultural community. Many families preserved their San identities clandestinely while living openly as AbaThembu, maintaining their clan names and giving their children historical anchors—such as naming a great-granduncle **Bushman**—to ensure that the memory of the mountain archers who stood at Quthing would never be erased from the earth.
